**Category:** Research and Analysis | **Year:** 2025

The dream is simple. Work calmly, with AI agents handling the repetitive parts, while I occasionally glance at the screen to check the output. That is where I am trying to get to. This is the story of how that obsession started.

---

## It Started With Excel, Not Code

In 2020 I spent a year at Universitas Brawijaya studying management before transferring to Universitas Indonesia. That year turned out to matter more than I expected — not because of the management curriculum, but because the coursework demanded real Excel proficiency. Accounting calculations, financial modeling, data tables. I started learning formulas on my own, and somewhere in the process I realized I genuinely enjoyed it.

What I was actually falling for was not Excel specifically. It was the logic underneath it: if this input, then that output. Conditional thinking. Structured problem-solving. I did not have a name for it then. I just knew I liked building things that worked automatically.

When I moved to Public Administration at UI the following year, I assumed that chapter was closed. A social science degree did not seem like the place for technical skills. That assumption was wrong.

---

## The Statistics Quiz Dashboard

Semester two brought a statistics course, and with it, weekly quizzes at the end of every session. I noticed the pattern early: the questions followed predictable structures, the calculations were repetitive, and the bottleneck was always computation speed rather than conceptual understanding.

So I built something.

![Statistics dashboard built in Excel, semester 2 at Universitas Indonesia](images/statistics-excel.jpg)

It was a dashboard in Excel — input your raw data, and the sheet automatically computed frequency distributions, measures of central tendency, variance, standard deviation, and qualitative variation indices. Before each class I would update the data and have everything ready. During quizzes I could answer faster and more accurately than would have been possible by hand.

This was before generative AI existed in any practical form. The method was just logic, formulas, and color-coded cells. But it worked, and more importantly, it taught me something: automating a repetitive task does not make you worse at understanding it. It frees your attention for the parts that actually require judgment.

---

## Computational Social Science and the Research Competitions

The statistics course opened a door. I started reading beyond the syllabus — meta-analysis, social network analysis, computational methods applied to social and governance questions. These were not taught in the curriculum at the time, but they connected directly to the policy and administrative questions I was studying.

I brought these methods into research competitions. Meta-analysis went into a paper on fishermen welfare programs for Brawijaya National Student Conference, which took first place. Social network analysis became the methodological core of my PKM-RSH research on juvenile correctional institutions, which reached PIMNAS and won a Silver Medal, and later formed the foundation of my undergraduate thesis.

Every one of those projects required the same discipline: map the workflow from input to output, identify what can be structured and automated, and focus human attention on the interpretive and analytical steps that cannot. I was doing that with macro recordings for data collection, structured templates for classification, and systematic pipelines for processing research data. The thinking was the same as the statistics dashboard. The tools were just more complex.

## Automation at Work

Professionally, the same instinct continues. I have built three workflow automation projects in my current role, and my workplace has been supportive, providing training on automation within the Google ecosystem, which aligned well with what I was already building on my own.

The clearest example is a regulatory monitoring pipeline. I set up Google Alerts for relevant topics, which generates daily digest emails. Those emails are automatically extracted and passed to Gemini for analysis, and the structured output is pushed to Google Chat as a daily intelligence update for the team. What previously required manual monitoring and write-up now runs on its own.

![Google Alerts Intelligence Analysis workflow built in Google Workspace Studio](images/google-alert-automation.jpg)

A second workflow handles stakeholder calendar management: tracking important dates, events, and relationship touchpoints that would otherwise require someone to remember and flag manually. Automated reminders surface the right information at the right time without anyone having to maintain a separate tracking system.

A third focuses on information aggregation, pulling updates from multiple sources into a single structured summary, reducing the time spent context-switching between channels to stay informed.

None of these replace judgment. They clear the space for it.

---

## What Is Next

The honest answer is that I have more ideas than time to build them. Every workflow I have automated has revealed two or three others that could be. That gap between what is possible and what has been built is not frustrating — it is motivating.

The direction is clear. More automation, better integration, and eventually a working environment where the routine is handled by systems I have designed, and my attention is reserved for the work that genuinely needs it. The AI agents are part of that picture. So is continuing to learn, both from what the tools can do and from what they cannot.

That version of the job is worth working toward.
