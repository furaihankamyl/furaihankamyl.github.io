**Category:** Speaking and Presentation | **Year:** 2025

Most of what determines whether a policy conversation goes well happens before anyone enters the room. By the time a meeting starts, the real work — understanding the issue, mapping who matters and why, deciding what to say and how to say it — has usually already been done. What happens in the room is often just the execution of a plan built somewhere else, usually at a desk.

I find it useful to think about this work through a framework from Young and Quinn (2012), which breaks advocacy into three components: a way into the process, the message and the activities built around it, and the messenger delivering it. It is a simple structure, but it maps closely onto how policy communication actually works in practice — and it gives me a way to talk about the discipline without making it sound more mysterious than it is.

---

## At the Desk: Where the Work Actually Begins

Every issue starts with understanding it properly, not just the surface position, but the regulatory mechanics, the competing interests, and where the genuine points of friction sit. This is the unglamorous part: reading, cross-referencing, building a mental model of the issue that is accurate rather than convenient.

From there, the work moves to mapping stakeholders, both at the institutional and individual level. An institution has a position, but the people inside it have histories, incentives, and relationships that shape how that position gets expressed and how much room there is to engage with it. Understanding both layers matters. A ministry's official stance and the way a specific director general has historically approached similar issues are not always the same thing, and treating them as identical is a common mistake.

Part of this mapping involves understanding how a stakeholder has positioned itself on related issues over time, including the pattern behind it. This kind of historical awareness is less about tracking any single statement and more about building a coherent sense of where an institution has consistently stood, where it has shifted, and why. That context shapes everything that follows.

Once the issue and the stakeholders are understood, the communication strategy follows naturally: what the core narrative should be, which framing will land with which audience, and which channel makes sense for which message, a formal media statement carries different weight than a direct conversation, and choosing wrong undermines the work that came before it.

All of this — the issue understanding, the stakeholder map, the narrative, becomes the capital you carry into every conversation that follows. None of it stays at the desk.

---

## Beyond the Desk: Where the Relationships Live

The preparation only matters if it translates into real engagement, and that engagement happens through relationships — formal and informal, built deliberately over time rather than assembled the moment they are needed.

This starts before any direct contact exists. Knowing who to reach out to, through which channel, with what framing, is itself a skill. It continues through the first meeting, where the goal is rarely to resolve anything immediately, and extends into the long, less visible work of maintaining a relationship across both professional and casual contexts — because trust built only in formal settings tends to be thinner than trust built across both.

Public communication operates on a parallel track. Media coverage serves a different function than direct stakeholder engagemen. It shapes how an institution's position is understood more broadly, by audiences who will never sit in a meeting but whose perception still matters. Knowing when to communicate through media versus through direct channels, and how to keep those two tracks coherent with each other, is part of the same discipline.

At a wider scale, this work sometimes involves building coalitions with industry peers around shared issues — recognizing that some positions carry more weight collectively than individually, and that alignment with other actors facing the same regulatory questions can shift how an issue is received.

## On the Limits of What I Have Done

I should be honest about where my experience sits within this picture. Much of my work has concentrated on the desk side of this framework — issue analysis, stakeholder mapping, and communication strategy — and on the public-facing dimensions of engagement. I have had less direct exposure to the more relational, in-person dimension of advocacy: the long-cultivated personal relationships built over years of informal contact.

I have studied that dimension closely, because understanding how it works is part of understanding the whole system, even in the parts where my own hands-on experience is still developing. I think that distinction matters. Claiming expertise in something you have only observed is different from being honest about what you have done versus what you understand conceptually, and I would rather be precise about that line than blur it.

What I keep coming back to is that advocacy work is rarely about a single persuasive moment. It is a system, the issue you understand, the stakeholders you have mapped, the message you have built, the relationships you maintain, and the channels you choose to use. Every successful interaction in the room is downstream of decisions made well before it and sustained well after it.

The room matters. But it is the smallest part of the work.
